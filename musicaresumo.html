How American Music from the 80s and 90s Affected Brazilian Politics


In the 80s and 90s, American music had a big impact on Brazil, both culturally and politically. During this time, Brazil was going through an important transition to democracy, which led to the creation of the Constitution in 1988. Music played a key role in expressing people's desires for freedom and equality, reflecting the political mood of the time.


One of the most notable songs was "Ebony and Ivory" by Paul McCartney and Stevie Wonder, which was a huge hit in 1982. The song became a powerful symbol for racial equality, a significant issue in Brazil due to its history of slavery and racial inequality. The chorus, "Ebony and ivory live together in perfect harmony," clearly conveyed the message that black and white people could and should live together in peace and equality. This message deeply resonated in a society trying to overcome past divisions and injustices, supporting the movement for democracy and civil rights.


Besides "Ebony and Ivory," other American songs also influenced Brazilian politics. "Fight the Power" by Public Enemy, released in the late 80s, tackled racism and oppression, impacting the hip hop movement in Brazil and becoming an anthem for those fighting against social injustice. "Killing in the Name" by Rage Against the Machine, in the 90s, criticized police brutality and institutional racism and was adopted as a protest anthem in Brazil.


"Born in the U.S.A." by Bruce Springsteen, though often misunderstood, criticized U.S. internal policies and served as a reminder of struggles for justice and democracy in Brazil. "Smells Like Teen Spirit" by Nirvana, while not directly political, captured the rebellious spirit of a generation questioning corruption and social inequality.


These songs showed how art can cross borders and influence different parts of the world. In Brazil, they helped shape the political views of a generation, inspiring young people to question authority and fight for a fairer country. American music from the 80s and 90s not only entertained but also educated and mobilized, leaving an important mark on Brazil's political history.
